# Does it work? The measures

Two things get measured here, kept apart on purpose: whether the **product** is any good, and whether the **way of working** is any good. Both are claims you can check rather than take on faith.

## Is the product good?

The honest test is whether people use it and whether it helps. The single clearest signal for the job tool is the **applied → in-process conversion rate** — of the roles the screener surfaces and someone applies to, how many turn into a real conversation. That measures whether the tool surfaces things that actually convert, not just things that look like matches. Alongside it: are people running it more than once, and do the roles it calls GO hold up on a human read.

## Is the way of working good?

This is the team-OS claim, and it's measured by what the method removes:

- **Time from clone to first contribution** — the headline. The whole "no meetings" promise rests on a newcomer getting from arriving to contributing inside one session. This gets clocked for every new contributor.
- **Handoffs that didn't need a meeting** — every time the build log carries a piece of work from one person to the next without a call, that's the method earning its keep. Tagged as it happens.
- **Context reuse** — how often the shared context answers a question instead of a human having to.
- **Participant sentiment** — whether contributors feel like builders or like lab subjects. This is the tripwire: if the method ever starts serving the experiment more than the people in it, that's a failure regardless of the other numbers.

## Baselines

Baselines get set in the open as the build starts, so the numbers above are measured against a stated starting point rather than a moving one. Until they're set, treat the measures as the things we're watching, not yet as results.
